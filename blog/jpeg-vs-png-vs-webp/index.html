JPEG vs PNG vs WebP — Which Image Format Should You Use?

June 15, 2026 · 4 min read · By CompressNow

Choosing the wrong image format can mean files 5× larger than necessary — slowing down your website, getting your email bounced, or making your app feel sluggish. Here's exactly when to use JPEG, PNG, or WebP.

When to Use JPEG

JPEG is still the safest choice when you need universal compatibility and reasonable file sizes. Use JPEG for:

  • Photographs — Portraits, landscapes, product photos. JPEG's compression is optimized for natural images.
  • Social media uploads — Every platform accepts JPEG. Instagram, Facebook, Twitter all recompress uploads anyway.
  • Email attachments — When you need to send a photo and want it to open on any device.
  • Legacy systems — Government portals, old CMS platforms, and some printers only accept JPEG.
Avoid JPEG for: Logos, icons, UI elements, text-heavy images, or anything that needs a transparent background. The compression creates visible artifacts around sharp edges.

When to Use PNG

PNG is the format for graphics that need to look perfect. Every pixel is preserved. Use PNG for:

  • Logos and brand assets — You want your logo to look crisp at every size. PNG's lossless compression ensures that.
  • Icons and UI elements — Buttons, favicons, app icons — anything with sharp edges and few colors.
  • Screenshots — PNG preserves text perfectly. JPEG makes text in screenshots look blurry.
  • Images with transparency — If you need a transparent background, PNG is your only widely-supported option (WebP also works but has 97% support).
Avoid PNG for: Photographs destined for the web. A 5MB PNG photo can often be converted to a 300KB JPEG with no visible quality loss.

When to Use WebP

WebP is the modern choice — smaller than JPEG at the same quality, supports transparency like PNG, and even handles animation. Use WebP for:

  • Website images — Google's own analysis shows WebP saves 25-35% vs JPEG. That directly improves Core Web Vitals and SEO.
  • E-commerce product photos — Smaller images = faster page loads = higher conversion rates.
  • Blog and content sites — If 97% browser support is enough for your audience, switch everything to WebP.
  • Apps and PWAs — WebP's small size is perfect for mobile, where every KB matters.
Avoid WebP for: Platforms that don't support WebP uploads (some government portals, older email clients, certain CMS). When in doubt, keep a JPEG fallback.

The Bottom Line

For most people in 2026, the priority order is: WebP > JPEG > PNG for photos, and PNG > WebP > JPEG for graphics with transparency. If you're unsure, compress in multiple formats and compare the file sizes — the difference can be dramatic.
